Service design overview

Shelter, the housing and homelessness charity

  • Date: March 2018. / Category: Service Design, UX Design

How can Service Design help make sure Shelter is there for people that need housing advice the most?

Being the first Service Designer at Shelter, the leading housing and homelessness charity in the UK, gave me the opportunity to share the potential of Service Design by helping deliver a substantial step forward in quality and quantity of the advice provided daily to thousands of people in need.

Overview




Shelter's free national helpline is open all year round, offering expert advice to anyone struggling with issues related to housing and homelessness.
In 2016/17 our helpline advisers spoke to 60,580 people, spending an average of 25 minutes talking to each person.
Our team, formed by a Service Designer, a UX Lead, a Web Developer and a Product Owner, adopted a ‘design thinking’ approach when looking at how we could help our users connect with the services we offer.

One of the main challenges our helpline faces is the large number of people who call us but are unable to get through to speak with an adviser. Demand for the service is much higher than the helpline’s capacity to answer calls.

Investigating the problem

We mapped the user journey and the system by talking to advisers, listening to calls, reading advice chatlogs; we looked at specific reports to get a broader understanding of the context in which we were operating and analysed data about our advice services demand.


By triangulating qualitative, quantitative and desk research we found out that:
› our service offering wasn't very clear to people in need
› it was not easy to self-serve information
› we were answering a large amount of non-urgent calls
› advice journeys were not consistent across touchpoints, making it hard for users to switch channel

What we did

We looked at the painpoints on the current user journey and broke this down into features/service components that would iron out the painpoints.
By combining them, we envisioned the ideal user journey (end to end service) and developed a roadmap.

Some solutions we’ve launched so far are:
› the now most visited 'Get Help' web page
› an Emergency Helpline that helped thousands of people being assisted by Shelter

So far we've streamlined the user journey and increased the percentage of emergency calls answered by the helpline. Next we’ll be looking to make it easier for users to self-serve advice content in cases where speaking with an advisor may not be necessary to solve their problem.

Consindering the size and long history of Shelter it's fascinating to see Service Design expressing its potential where it can clearly deliver positive change.
