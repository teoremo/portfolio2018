Streamlining web users's housing advice journey

Shelter's Get Help page

  • Date: June 2017. / Category: Service Design, UX Design

In 2017 at Shelter we began to think about why people need access to our services and how they access them. We wanted to find out if there was anything we could do to improve the way our website users find the help they need.

Researching the problem

We started by looking at in-depth, quality research produced by our researchers and some trend analysis. We then considered the expectations internet users would normally have for the kind of services we provide, and validated our assumptions with user testing.

We came up with some fundamental questions to help us better understand how people access our services and test our assumptions.

We asked:
› how do people find out about Shelter?
› how do people prefer to access advice?
› why do people prefer to interact with an adviser despite visiting our advice pages?
› do we know how many of our service users prefer to call the Helpline, use webchat or see someone face-to-face?

With information gathered from existing analytics and surveys we learnt that most of the people who call our helpline have visited Shelter's website before.

What did we learn?

The majority of our service users come to our website unsure of what they are looking for. While they know they have a problem and want help, they may not know what kind of help is available to them.

For example, someone might be being threatened with eviction because they can’t afford their rent following an increase. They might then come to our website for advice following an internet search or recommendation from a friend. If the person is uncomfortable talking about their problem on the phone, they might search for a way to chat to one of our advisers online.

Getting advice through our web chat service was only possible if a pop-up appeared on one of the pages in the advice section of our website. This made it difficult for users to find this service, even though it was an option.

The Solution

We proposed a new ‘Get help’ page to act as one clear, unified entry point for users trying to access any of our services. 


This would make it possible for us to:
› create a clear path for any advice journey, with the ability to introduce and test different advice options and evaluate their impact
› allow users to make a choice based on their own preference on how to access help
› provide clear alternatives to users if they are not able to access one of our services. For example, encouraging users to call the helpline if they don’t have face-to-face services available in their local area

The results

Since the ‘Get Help’ page has been live it has become the most visited page on our website, after the home page. We now see almost 90% of all web chats come through it - while the remaining 10% is actively prompted by the team on specific high-risk pages.

As we ‘design for data’, we always think about ways to measure usage and outcomes. We are now able to analyse people’s journeys on our website with improved accuracy and have been monitoring the impact of the experimental 'emergency helpline'.

We have received positive feedback about the range of contact options available. One of our users said: ‘There was lots of ways to talk to people directly, live chat phone numbers and walk-in centers across the country (...) so lots of options.’

The ‘Get help’ page has also been useful for members of staff in their day-to-day work. Spike Witney, our social media officer, said: ‘The benefits for social are huge. Now we can direct people to this page in one comment or tweet, rather than a series explaining how they can access each service depending on their need. Early results are very positive and it's a clean, modern way to filter advice questions.’

Continuing to improve

Following the introduction of the ‘Get help’ page, webchat has now been enabled for users accessing our website through a mobile device. The number of web chat advisers has also doubled.
We will continue to improve the ‘Get help’ page to match the changing needs of our service users.
