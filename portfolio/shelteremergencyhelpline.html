Increasing the amount of emergency calls answered by advisers

Shelter's Emergency Helpline

  • Date: June 2017. / Category: Service Design, UX Design

As people's housing enquiries can be very varied – ranging from minor landlord disputes to homeless families facing a night on the streets – we decided to look at how we could make sure Shelter's advisers are helping callers with the most urgent need.
This would also give us the opportunity to look at how we can improve our self-help services – such as our digital advice webpages – for users who do not necessarily need to speak directly with an adviser.

Researching the problem

Through our research we realised that at Shelter we were currently looking at ‘emergency’ situations from a legal perspective, but this meant we weren’t considering people’s individual situations and feelings around their need for help.

For example, we came across several examples of people who called our helpline because they were struggling with housing problems which might usually be considered as non-urgent or easy to solve issues. However, due to personal circumstances – such as learning difficulties or issues with mental health – the callers saw their problem as a much bigger, more urgent issue. As a result, they needed to speak with an adviser directly.

As we started to consider this new way of looking at things, we began to work with the Telephone and Online Advice Services team and the helpline advisers to come up with a new list of criteria to help us assess when calls are urgent.



We decided a caller’s situation would be classed as an ‘emergency’ when:
› the person feels extremely overwhelmed about their housing situation
› the person has nowhere to sleep, or might be homeless soon
› the person has somewhere to sleep, but nowhere to call home
› the person is/could be at risk of harm
This new criteria helped us to consider the impact of housing problems in individual situations, rather than just focusing on legal definitions. This also meant we could adjust any policies which might risk excluding people who fall outside of the legal definitions.

We then looked at a week’s worth of data from our helpline advisers to help us understand the kind of calls we were currently answering. This data showed us that only around 23% of the calls answered by our helpline advisers were considered ‘emergency’ situations.This gave us a baseline metric we could use in any further testing we planned to run.

During our research we also looked at other situations in which a person might be a priority for help. This helped us to find the two most common solutions to our problem. Firstly, we considered the emergency services, who rely on their call handlers to triage patients and decide their level of need. Secondly, we considered public situations, such as priority seating on the tube or spaces in a car park where it is the responsibility of individuals to decide if they consider themselves to be in need of these priority resources.

Our Solution

As part of the 'Get Help' page launch, we decided to run an experiment where we would have a dedicated emergency line to the helpline.
This number would take callers to the front of the helpline queue, and the first available adviser would then pick up the call. This would effectively give people with urgent issues priority over other callers
This number would appear on the helpline page and have clear guidelines on what would be considered an emergency, so users understand when they could call this number.


Over about a two month period we were able to run two different versions of our experiment. We took our learnings from the first version and applied these to the second run.

The results

By the end of a third round of experiments, data from our helpline advisers' showed us some interesting results. We learnt:

› we now serve about 30% emergency calls (increased from the initial 23%)
› callers who felt they should be put through to an adviser as a priority call are three times more likely to have their call answered.
› currently around 70% of the calls to the emergency helpline are genuinely emergency cases, that are getting through much faster
› the second most common reason callers needed emergency help was that they simply felt overwhelmed by their housing situation – showing our updated ‘emergency’ criteria really made an impact

Continuing to improve

We faced several challenges during this project, including how to restrict callers from using the emergency helpline number to wrongly ‘skip the queue’. We will continue to learn from the results of the emergency number and will continue to grow the potential of this project.

The success of this project has been thanks to collaboration with the Telephone and Online Advice Services team and our helpline advisers. We will continue to work together with the helpline and people who use our services in future versions of this project.
